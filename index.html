  • The Pop Words Project

    Right wing populist leaders have sprung across the world, unprecedently amassing millions of fans and followers. These leaders are thought to share very similar ways of communicating with their intended audiences. But exactly what political strategies and rhetorics underlie their popularity?

    Pop Words tackles these questions by analyzing a corpus of speeches by populist right-wing leaders like Trump, Salvini and Bolsonaro. We work in the domain of political rhetoric analysis through close and distant reading methods such as rhetorical device analysis, topic modelling, sentiment analysis, and lexical space (complexity) analysis. We then create a formal ontology to conceptualize and formalize rhetorical strategies employed by Right wing populists (RWPs). While we first analyze and formalize the speech acts of RWPs, this ontology can be exploited for a variety of political content and speech acts, regardless of political affiliation.

  • The Initial Corpus

    In order to develop an ontology of political rhetorics, we decided to start by focusing on speech acts by three of the most popular RWP leaders of the moment: President Donald Trump of the US, (Ex) Minister of the Interior Matteo Salvini of Italy, and President Jair Bolsonaro of Brazil.

    Specifically, we decided to build the ontology based on data gathered from close and distant reading of a small corpus made of speeches by these three leaders. The speeches selected include:

    • Trump’s Inaugural Address (January 20th, 2017, Washington DC, USA)

Cleaning the Data

    In order to analyze our corpus through distant reading methods, we had to first clean the data. The algorithm works through the following steps:
    1. The algorithm opens every single file of our corpus (.txt)
    2. Our data are processed by using the pickle module, which is defined as follows: “The pickle module implements binary protocols for serializing and de-serializing a Python object structure. “Pickling” is the process whereby a Python object hierarchy is converted into a byte stream, and “unpickling” is the inverse operation, whereby a byte stream (from a binary file or bytes-like object) is converted back into an object hierarchy. Pickling (and unpickling) is alternatively known as “serialization”, “marshalling,” or “flattening”; however, to avoid confusion, the terms used here are “pickling” and “unpickling”.”
    3. The data-cleaning phase is then divided into two sub-phases:
    3a. Make text lowercase, remove text in square brackets, remove punctuation and remove words containing numbers
    3b. Get rid of some additional punctuation and nonsensical text that was missed the first time around.
    4. The data are saved in different formats, in order to be used for different purposes, and a Document Term Matrix is created and saved on the go.
    Everything is exported in a pickle file (.pkl). This is the list of the file exported:

Sentiment Analysis

We performed sentiment analysis to see what the emotional valence of the speeches was.

First of all, we analyzed the sentiment for every speech by using an easy-to-use library for Python called AYLIEN. This tool returns a sentiment analysis in natural language (positive, negative, neutral, etc). You can find the Python code below.

We then wanted something more sophisticated, that could provide us with a more complex and comparable result. We decided to use TextBlob, which -differently from Aylien - can return numeric results both for the sentiment and for the objectivity. In particular, the results of this tool are in the range:
Polarity: How positive or negative a word is. -1 is very negative. +1 is very positive.
Subjectivity: How subjective, or opinionated a word is. 0 is fact. +1 is very much an opinion.
